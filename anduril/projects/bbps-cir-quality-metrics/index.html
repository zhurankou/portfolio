FINAL BBPS & CIR DESIGNS

01 // Contribution

My design partner and I redesigned the Boston Bowel Preparation Score (BBPS) and Cecal Intubation Rate (CIR) pages in OlySense Clinical Insights. I led the CIR design while she owned BBPS, making these critical colonoscopy quality metrics easier for endoscopists and endoscopy leads to understand and act on.

Together we led product design across the full process — synthesizing evaluation research, shaping low‑ and high‑fidelity concepts, partnering with UX Research on two rounds of studies, and iterating the charts and tables based on clinician feedback.

Initial evaluations showed BBPS and CIR were among the most valuable metrics but hardest to interpret. Less tech‑savvy directors struggled with BBPS column meanings. For CIR, users couldn't easily see cecal reach rates or understand why intubation failed.

02 // Low‑Fidelity Concept Testing

My design partner and I worked with UX Research to define concept‑testing goals and build low‑fi prototypes. She explored BBPS dashboard vs simpler charts; I tested CIR line charts with different hover drills (Extent vs Reasons cecum not reached).

We ran 60‑minute remote interviews with clinicians across two hospitals, using four concepts.

Key Learnings — BBPS

Focus on adequate vs low scores, not full score distributions.
Segment breakdown essential for 0/1 scores indicating incomplete procedures.
Simpler charts beat dense dashboards.
"BBPS by doctor" didn't make sense — bowel prep reflects patient preparation, not endoscopist performance.

Design directions from low‑fi: Define "passing" BBPS more clearly — either as total ≥ 6 or at least 2 per segment. Group total BBPS into "under 6" vs "6 and over" for at‑a‑glance views. For CIR, carry forward the line chart with ESGE benchmark lines and add data about reasons cecum wasn't reached.

Key Learnings — CIR

Simple line chart of CIR over time matched clinicians' mental model for tracking trends.
"Extent of Colonoscopy" did not help explain low CIR — users couldn't tell if reaching each landmark was intended.
Showing reasons for not reaching cecum (elongated colon, stenosis) was perceived as much more actionable.

03 // High‑Fidelity Designs & Usability

Using our OlySense design system, my design partner built hi‑fi BBPS prototypes while I created CIR designs. Together we partnered with UX Research on usability testing focused on core questions: "Are bowel preps adequate?", "Which segments score low?", "What's my CIR vs ESGE?", "Why no cecum reach?"

We co‑authored the research plan, discussion guide, and tasks, then co‑moderated 7 sixty‑minute remote interviews with clinicians across multiple hospitals. We observed how easily they completed tasks like finding inadequate BBPS months, identifying segments with low scores, and retrieving CIR per doctor.

Key Findings — BBPS

"% BBPS scores ≥ 6" chart was very helpful — all doctors understood their success rate and identified the lowest month.
"Adequate" needed to explicitly say "at least 2 per segment" so users could trust the calculation.
Terminology like "blocked" for score 0 was flagged as clinically incorrect — users wanted ESGE-aligned language.

Design decisions from hi‑fi: Keep the "average adequate scores over time" BBPS chart but clarify adequacy definition. Make ESGE benchmark lines more visually distinct and show values above data points. For CIR, reinforce ESGE threshold lines and emphasize reasons for not reaching cecum in both hover and supporting tables.

Key Findings — CIR

Participants easily found CIR scores meeting ESGE guidelines and identified months below target.
"Cecum not reached" table broken down by reason was considered very helpful for understanding whether low CIR was patient factors or technique.
Endoscopy leads valued "CIR by doctor" to see which doctors struggled with cecal intubation.

04 // Final Design

My design partner's BBPS contribution: "% BBPS scores ≥ 6" chart with explicit adequacy definition, plus segment view highlighting risky 0/1 procedures without overwhelming the page.

My CIR contribution: Clean line chart with ESGE thresholds, prominent KPI, and "Cecum not reached" table showing actionable reasons (not just Extent reached). Leads get CIR by doctor with context to prioritize coaching.

Together, these designs make BBPS and CIR easier to scan, interpret, and act on for clinicians with varying levels of technical comfort, while aligning terminology and thresholds with ESGE and local practice.

What I'd Explore Next

Side‑by‑side year or period comparison views (e.g., this year vs last year) to help teams see the impact of new prep protocols or training.
Filters for excluding legitimate non‑reach cases in CIR (e.g., severe stenosis), so rates more accurately reflect controllable quality.
